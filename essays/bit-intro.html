A little bit about bits

Coding bootcamps and self-study programs are usually light on bits and binary operations.

It makes sense. You have limited time, and most of the time there is enough abstraction to effectively program and build things without needing to get to the level of the bit.

However, this gap in knowledge can make a big difference in your coding interviews as well as your job performance.

Computer science grads learn all about bits and binary operations and your understanding or lack thereof in this area can be used as a signal to recruiters, interviewers and your boss about the depth of your computer science knowledge.

Plus, depending on your role, bits can play an important role in the software you develop. They are used to optimize memory and performance in numerous ways.

We'll get into the weeds on bits here in this article, but it's crucial to understand that bits are important for passing your coding interviews, performing well on the job and simply being a better, more competent software engineer.

The fundamentals

What are bits?

A bit (aka "binary digit") is the most basic unit of storage in a computer. It only has two possible states. Most commonly, we represent these states as either 0 or 1.

You can also represent them as true/false, off/on and so on, but let's stick with 0s and 1s to align with the most common bit terminology.

All software boils down to bits. Every image, software application and piece of data in your computer is stored only in 0s and 1s.

Under the hood, there is a physical mechanism in the computer that changes between the two states.

There are various ways of physically storing bits, but generally bits are implemented through microscopic pieces of hardware called transistors.

Transistors act like switches and can be turned on (1) or off (0) depending on the amount of voltage being passed through them.

Pretty cool right?

Bytes

It gets a little unwieldy to talk about storage in terms of individual bits. It's easier to think in terms of collections of bits, the smallest of which is called a byte.

A byte is merely a contiguous series of 8 bits. In most computers, a byte is the smallest addressable unit of storage in a computer.

By addressable, I mean it's the smallest unit of memory that has an address the Central Processing Unit (CPU) can use to locate data.

The CPU uses this address when it needs to perform any kind of operation for this particular byte. In other words, it's how the computer finds stuff the that you stored in a sea of bits.

I'm simplifying a tad (we can get into encoding in a different post), but a byte can hold a single character like "a" or "B".

We can use a byte to store several different patterns of bits that represent various values.

Base-2 Number System

To understand how bytes are used to represent values through bits, you have to understand how the base-2 number system works.

We're used to a base-10 number system. In the most basic sense, this means that each digit of a number is multiplied by a certain power of 10, depending on its position.
